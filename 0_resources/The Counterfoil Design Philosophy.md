# The Counterfoil Design Philosophy

---

All design philosophies must answer one question for designing objects: 

> How is "good design" defined?

"Good" design is always about "fit," a "good" design "fits" into its context and serves its function well. Therefore, answering the question "what is good" is begging a deeper and more elaborate question:

> What assumptions do we have for the utility and context of this object?

The Counterfoil Design Philosophy is an opinionated document that discusses what "good design" is by outlining our assumptions for users, utility, and context. Its goal is to provide design principles that can be used when designing convivial tools. 







The broad goal of Counterfoil is to "to devise tools and tool systems that optimize the balance of life, thereby maximizing liberty for all." 

> Convivial Tools are approachable, understandable, and controllable to many people in society. They serve humans and communities (rather than force humans to serve them). 

We do not assume that humans are simple, passive consumers. 

Instead, we assume a Jeffersonian vision where yeoman software developers are able to inspect, fix, and maintain software as community commons tools. "Convivial Design" means that these yeomen are able to easily understand the software that they're looking at. 

Yeomen Software developers are neighborhood, community software developers, are not specialists. They are everyday professional developers or advanced hobbyists. They do not have PhDs or genius depths of understanding of computer science. They can be produced by bootcamps or online tutorials and their understanding grows through experience. They are the kinds of developers that you can find in any major city in any city on any continent. 



Counterfoil Design Philosophy believes that a "good" design is something that:

1. Empowers its end users, and...
2. Can be easily serviced by Yeoman developers



### Specific qualities of Convivial Tooling:

- **Common, Community Tooling** - tools rely on popular, well-maintained, open-source libraries and build systems. This means yeoman developers (not specialists) can easily understand and maintain them. It also ensures that tools can be maintained long term because they are no reliant on systems that will not be up-kept. 
- **Interoperability** - Wherever possible, convivial tools do not lock themselves in to one type of technology. Systems should be composable, so if a dependency stops being supported, it can be easily replaced. 
- **Open data formats** - Yeomen who work on convivial tools understand that even these tools might not last forever. As a service to their users, tools interact with standard file types as much as possible (markdown files, standard image files, simple yamls, etc...) so that, should the application stop being supported, users will not lose their data. 
- **Data location is visible** - We believe that the web2 is immoral because it hides the location of user's data in the mysterious "cloud" where it smashes together identity, front-end application, and file storage into one opaque "experience." Counterfoil believes that application and data should be rendered as separate for users so that they can always understand that "data" "lives" somewhere, and that applications are tools that act on that data. 
- **Inspectability over Elegance** - We do not seek the most elegant or efficient solution. Instead, we want to build tools that are human readable for general yeoman developers.
